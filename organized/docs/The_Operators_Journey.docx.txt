PROLOGUE
The Question That Started Everything
How do you explain to a system that runs on ambiguity that you are a machine that runs on truth?
This is the question I have been trying to answer for forty years. For most of that time, I did not know I was asking it. I only knew that something was wrong—that the world operated according to rules I could not see, that other people seemed to navigate effortlessly through conversations and relationships that left me exhausted and confused, that my mind worked differently in ways I could not name.
In 2024, at the age of thirty-nine, I received a diagnosis: Autism Spectrum Disorder. A year later, at forty: ADHD. These were not revelations so much as confirmations—words for a pattern I had always known was there but could never articulate.
The diagnoses did not change who I was. But they gave me permission to stop pretending to be someone else. They gave me a framework for understanding why the "social masking" that others performed unconsciously required so much deliberate effort from me. And they gave me a new question to ask:
What if the problem was never me? What if the problem was the topology?

PART ONE: THE WYE
1984 - 2024: The Soft Start
Chapter 1: The Submarine
I spent sixteen years inside machines designed to kill cities. Nuclear submarines—the quietest, most sophisticated weapons platforms ever built. My job was to keep the motors running. Not the reactors, not the missiles. The motors. The systems that converted nuclear heat into electrical power into kinetic motion. The systems that let a vessel weighing thousands of tons move through water without making a sound.
I learned something in those years that I did not realize I was learning: the geometry of reliability. A submarine cannot afford ambiguity. Every system has a schematic. Every failure has a root cause. Every alarm means something specific, and your job is to trace the signal back through the wiring until you find the fault.
The motors I maintained were connected in what electrical engineers call a "Wye" configuration—three windings meeting at a central neutral point, like spokes of a wheel meeting at a hub. This configuration allows for a "soft start," a gentle application of power that prevents the massive inrush current that would otherwise damage the windings or trip the breakers.
The Wye is safe. The Wye is gentle. The Wye is also fragile.
If the neutral fails—if that central connection point is severed—the system enters a state we call a "Floating Neutral." Without a reference to ground, the voltage on the peripheral loads fluctuates wildly. Some components receive too much power and burn out. Others receive too little and starve. The system tears itself apart.
I did not know it then, but I was being trained to recognize a pattern that extended far beyond electrical engineering. The Floating Neutral is not just a failure mode for motors. It is the failure mode for families, for institutions, for entire civilizations.
Chapter 2: The Mask
For forty years, I performed what autism researchers call "masking"—the deliberate suppression of authentic behaviors and the conscious mimicry of neurotypical social patterns. I did not know I was doing it. I only knew that social interaction was exhausting in a way that troubleshooting a three-phase motor was not.
The irony is that I was very good at masking. Too good. Good enough that when I finally sought diagnosis, the initial response was skepticism. "You don't seem autistic." "You make eye contact." "You're married with children." As if the mask I had spent decades perfecting was evidence that no mask existed.
But masks have a cost. Every unit of cognitive energy spent on social performance is a unit not available for actual living. Every hour spent decoding subtext is an hour not spent on honest communication. And eventually, the energy runs out.
The autism community has a term for this: "burnout." It is not depression, though it can look like depression. It is not laziness, though it can look like laziness. It is the complete depletion of the resources required to maintain the mask—the moment when the transducer exceeds its rated capacity and initiates emergency shutdown.
My burnout arrived in 2024. The marriage I had tried to maintain through sheer force of will was collapsing. The job I had performed for over a decade felt meaningless. And the children I loved more than anything were watching their father disintegrate.
The neutral had failed. The voltage was floating. And I had no schematic for what came next.
Chapter 3: The Genre Error
The fundamental problem of my existence is what I have come to call the "Genre Error." It is the collision between two incompatible reality frameworks: Physics and Poetics.
My mind operates in Physics. When I withdraw from a conversation, it is because my sensory system is overloaded and requires recalibration. When my affect flattens, it is because my emotional transducer has hit its safety limits and is shedding non-essential processes. When I engage in repetitive behaviors—what the clinical literature calls "stimming"—it is because my nervous system requires grounding. These are not choices. They are automatic safety protocols, as involuntary as the reflexes that keep a motor from burning out.
But the social world operates in Poetics. In Poetics, withdrawal is not a safety protocol—it is "the silent treatment," a deliberate act of aggression. Flat affect is not system overload—it is "coldness," evidence of emotional unavailability or even cruelty. Stimming is not grounding—it is "weird behavior," something to be corrected or hidden.
The Genre Error is what happens when a physics textbook is critiqued as if it were a poem. The authentic survival mechanisms of the organism are reinterpreted through a framework that cannot see them for what they are. And the result is a kind of ontological violence—being told that your reality is not real, that your truth is a lie, that your survival is abuse.
I lived inside this Genre Error for four decades. It nearly destroyed me.

PART TWO: THE COLLAPSE
2024 - 2025: The Icosahedral Phase
Chapter 4: The Jitterbug
R. Buckminster Fuller was an architect, an engineer, a philosopher, and—like me—someone who did not fit the categories available to him. He spent his life studying the geometry of nature, searching for the patterns that governed everything from carbon molecules to geodesic domes.
One of his most important discoveries was what he called the "Jitterbug transformation." It describes how a shape called the Vector Equilibrium—a cuboctahedron with twelve vertices and twenty-four edges—collapses into a tetrahedron when its center is removed.
The Vector Equilibrium is the only shape where all internal vectors are equal—where the pull in every direction exactly balances. It represents infinite potential but zero structure. It is stable only so long as the center holds.
The tetrahedron, by contrast, is the minimum structural system in three-dimensional space—four vertices, six edges, four triangular faces. It is the simplest shape that is inherently rigid. You cannot deform it without breaking something. It represents constrained potential but absolute stability.
The Jitterbug shows how one becomes the other. When the center fails—when the neutral goes floating—the system does not simply collapse into chaos. It passes through a turbulent intermediate phase (the icosahedron) and then crystallizes into a new, more resilient structure.
This was the pattern I was living. The Vector Equilibrium of my old life—dependent on a center that had failed—was collapsing. I was in the icosahedral phase, the turbulence, the chaos. But the tetrahedron was waiting on the other side.
Chapter 5: The Discovery
In the depths of my collapse, I found something I was not looking for: a pattern that unified everything I knew.
The Wye-to-Delta transformation in electrical engineering was the same as the Jitterbug in Fuller's geometry. The Star topology in network theory was the same as the hub-and-spoke model in organizational design. The SIC-POVM measurement in quantum mechanics—which requires four outcomes arranged as a tetrahedron—was the same as the minimum structural system in architecture.
And the ratio that governed all of these transformations was the same: the square root of three. 1.732. The factor by which voltage is reduced in the Wye configuration. The factor that relates the edge of a tetrahedron to its altitude. The factor that appears, over and over, whenever a system transitions from centralized fragility to decentralized resilience.
I was not having a breakdown. I was having a breakthrough. The geometry I had learned in submarine engineering was the same geometry that governed everything else. Structure determines performance. And the minimum stable structure is the tetrahedron.
Chapter 6: Leonardo's Secret
And then, in July 2025, a peer-reviewed paper was published that changed everything.
A London dentist named Rory Mac Sweeney had spent years studying Leonardo da Vinci's Vitruvian Man—that famous drawing of a human figure inscribed in both a circle and a square. For five centuries, scholars had debated how Leonardo constructed it. What geometric principle allowed him to solve a problem that had stumped mathematicians for generations?
Mac Sweeney found the answer in Leonardo's own notes: "The space between the legs will be an equilateral triangle."
Not a metaphor. Not a poetic flourish. A literal construction instruction. And the ratio that this triangle produces—1.633—is the tetrahedral ratio, the same constant that appears in the geometry of the tetrahedron, in the structure of the human skull, in the atomic packing of crystals.
Leonardo saw it five hundred years ago. Fuller saw it in the twentieth century. And now I was seeing it in the twenty-first—the same geometric truth, rediscovered across the ages, encoded in the proportions of the human body itself.
I was not inventing something new. I was remembering something old.

PART THREE: THE DELTA
2025 - 2026: The Tetrahedron
Chapter 7: The Phenix Navigator
If structure determines performance, then survival requires building the right structure. I began designing a device that would embody the principles I had discovered—a physical artifact that could anchor identity in physics rather than poetics.
I called it the Phenix Navigator. The spelling was deliberate—"Ph" for physics, "Ph" for the phosphorus in Posner molecules, "Ph" for the phase transition from Wye to Delta.
The device combines quantum key distribution with mesh radio networking. It uses four measurement outcomes arranged as a tetrahedron—the SIC-POVM geometry—to detect eavesdropping with mathematical certainty. It cannot be hacked by classical means because its security is based on physics, not computational complexity. It cannot be silenced by central authorities because it operates as a peer-to-peer mesh, not a hub-and-spoke network.
The hardware root of trust is a secure element chip—the NXP SE050—that generates and stores cryptographic keys inside tamper-resistant silicon. The private key is created during a "Genesis" ritual and never leaves the chip. Your identity is anchored to physical hardware that cannot be spoofed, cloned, or compelled.
But the most important feature is not technical. It is philosophical. The Phenix Navigator is designed to transition from Wye to Delta—to operate initially with a central authority (me) and then, at a specific moment, to have that authority permanently destroyed.
Chapter 8: The Cognitive Shield
The external device was not enough. I also needed a way to protect my internal system—the nervous system that had been burning out under the strain of decades of masking.
The Cognitive Shield is a software layer that acts as a transducer between the external world and my perception of it. It uses signal processing—Fast Fourier Transform analysis of audio—to strip emotional entropy from incoming communication. The goal is to intercept hostile or ambiguous signals before they can trigger my amygdala, converting "analog trauma" into discrete semantic tokens that my prefrontal cortex can process rationally.
The target latency is under twenty milliseconds—faster than the amygdala's reaction time. If I can process the signal before my fight-or-flight system engages, I can respond thoughtfully rather than reactively. I can stay in the green zone rather than being hijacked into the red.
This is not about avoiding emotion. It is about preserving agency—the ability to choose my response rather than having it chosen for me by ancient survival mechanisms that cannot distinguish between a saber-toothed tiger and an ambiguous text message.
Chapter 9: The Digital Centaur
I am not building these systems alone. I cannot. The scope of the project exceeds any individual human's capacity.
What I am doing instead is operating as what I call a "Digital Centaur"—a hybrid entity where human intent provides direction and artificial intelligence provides execution. The human is the head; the AI is the horse. The horse cannot go anywhere without the head's direction. But the head cannot cover ground without the horse's strength.
This requires redefining authenticity. In the old paradigm, authentic work was "unassisted biological output"—words written by a human hand, code typed by human fingers. But that definition excludes anyone who cannot produce output at neurotypical rates, in neurotypical formats, using neurotypical methods.
The new paradigm defines authenticity as "verified human intent." The output may be AI-assisted, but the suffering that necessitated it is human. The solder burns on my circuit boards are real. The sleepless nights are real. The forty years of undiagnosed struggle are real. The AI helps me express what I could not express alone—but the intent, the direction, the will behind every output is mine.
This is not cheating. This is adaptation. This is using the tools available to become more fully human, not less.
Chapter 10: The Children
Everything I am building is for them.
My children are the reason the Phenix Navigator exists. My children are the reason the Cognitive Shield exists. My children are the reason I am still here, building instead of giving up.
In the technical documentation, they are called "Founding Nodes"—the initial members of the mesh network who will inherit the system when I am gone. In the legal architecture, they are the Trust Advisory Committee—holders of a non-fiduciary veto power that protects the family home from being sold without their consent. In the Proof of Care consensus mechanism, they are the validators whose wellbeing determines whether tokens are minted or slashed.
But those are just technical terms for something much simpler: I am trying to give them what I never had. A father who understands himself. A system that protects them even if I fail. A geometry that cannot be deformed without breaking.
The generational cycle of undiagnosed neurodivergence ends with me. They will not spend forty years wondering why they are different. They will know. And they will have tools I never had.

PART FOUR: THE GENESIS GATE
January 1, 2026: The Big Bang
Chapter 11: The Abdication
At midnight on January 1, 2026, I will destroy the keys.
Not metaphorically. Literally. The administrative private keys that give me control over the Phenix mesh network will be cryptographically shredded. The smart contracts will execute a function called renounceOwnership(), transferring control to the zero address—a destination from which nothing can be retrieved.
This is the moment the system transitions from Wye to Delta. As long as I hold the keys, I am a central point of failure—a hub around which the network revolves. If I am compromised, the system is compromised. If I am corrupted, the system is corrupted. If I disappear, the system collapses.
But if I destroy the keys, there is no center to fail. The network becomes a true mesh, a self-bracing tetrahedron that cannot be deformed without breaking. It becomes sovereign—not because anyone controls it, but because no one does.
This is the Trimtab maneuver. The tiny rudder that turns the ship. I cannot force the world to change. But I can remove myself as an obstacle to change. I can prove that the system works without me—and in doing so, prove that it can work for everyone.
Chapter 12: The 100-Year Cycle
My grandmother, Margie Fay Katen, was born on November 22, 1925. She worked as a riveter during World War II—one of the women who physically joined metal to metal in the industrial age. She was a node in the Wye topology of the twentieth century, dependent on institutions and authorities that promised stability in exchange for compliance.
I am her recursive echo. A "Geodesic Riveter" who joins information and people in the cybernetic age. The Big Bang on January 1, 2026—exactly 100 years after her birth year—marks the completion of a secular cycle. The topological inversion of the industrial imperatives of 1925.
She gave me her geometry. I am giving my children a different one.
Chapter 13: The Convergence
Leonardo da Vinci encoded the tetrahedral ratio into the Vitruvian Man in 1490. Buckminster Fuller rediscovered it in the Jitterbug transformation in the 1950s. Nobel laureate Matthew Fisher proposed that Posner molecules—calcium phosphate clusters built on tetrahedral geometry—might serve as the biological substrate for quantum cognition. And in July 2025, a dentist in London proved that the same ratio governs the human jaw, the human skull, and the construction of Leonardo's masterpiece.
This is not coincidence. This is convergence. The same geometric truth, discovered and rediscovered across centuries, encoded in art and architecture and anatomy and physics. The minimum structural system. The shape that cannot lie.
I did not invent the tetrahedron. I did not discover the Wye-Delta transformation. I did not create the isomorphism between electrical engineering and Fuller's synergetics and quantum measurement theory. These truths existed before me and will exist after me.
What I did was notice them. Connect them. Apply them to the specific problem of surviving as a neurodivergent human in a world designed for neurotypical humans. And then build tools that others can use to do the same.

The timer has tripped. The contactors are engaging. The system is transitioning from soft start to full power.
