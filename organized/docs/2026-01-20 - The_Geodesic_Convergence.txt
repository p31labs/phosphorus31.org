The Star-Delta Transformation: A Primer
In 1899, Arthur Edwin Kennelly formalized what electrical engineers now call the Wye-Delta transformation. The principle is straightforward and has been verified billions of times in industrial applications worldwide.
A large induction motor cannot be switched on to full power instantaneously. The inrush current would be catastrophic—potentially damaging the windings, tripping breakers, or destabilizing the grid. The solution is the Star-Delta Starter.
Phase 1: The Star (Wye) Configuration. The motor windings are connected in a Y-shape, meeting at a central neutral point. The system is stable but dependent on that central neutral point.
The Timer. Once the motor reaches approximately 80% of synchronous speed, a timer trips.

This is not metaphor. This is how every large industrial motor on Earth operates.
The Failure Mode: What Happens When the Neutral Fails
In a Wye-connected system, the neutral wire serves a critical function: it carries imbalance current to ground. If loads on the three phases are unequal—which they always are in practice—the neutral absorbs the difference.
If the neutral wire is severed or disconnected—a condition called a "Floating Neutral"—the reference to ground is lost. The voltages on the peripheral loads begin to fluctuate unpredictably. A light bulb rated for 120V might suddenly receive 180V and explode. A computer rated for the same might receive 60V and starve.
This is not theoretical. Floating neutral conditions cause house fires, equipment destruction, and electrocution deaths every year. The National Electrical Code dedicates significant attention to preventing this exact failure mode.

The Measurement: SIC-POVMs and Quantum Tomography

This is the "fairness constant"—the geometric sweet spot that enables complete state reconstruction without redundancy.

The Network: Mesh Topology and Graph Curvature
In network theory, the distinction between Star (hub-and-spoke) and Mesh (peer-to-peer) topologies is fundamental. A Star network routes all traffic through a central hub. It is efficient but fragile: if the hub fails, the entire network fails. A Mesh network connects nodes directly to each other. It is less efficient but robust: no single point of failure can bring down the system.
Ollivier-Ricci curvature provides a mathematical framework for analyzing network resilience. Regions of positive curvature ("gravity wells") represent dense, well-connected clusters—traffic flows easily through them. Regions of negative curvature ("bottlenecks") represent fragile bridges—removing them disconnects the network. Optimal routing algorithms can use curvature to find resilient paths.
This is standard network science. The algorithms are deployed in production systems worldwide.
