III. The Tetrahedron Protocol: Cryptographic Logic
The Geodesic Engine implements the Tetrahedron Protocol, a specific QKD scheme developed by Renes, Blume-Kohout, Scott, and Caves. This protocol utilizes the "spherical code" geometry to outperform standard schemes in sifting efficiency and noise tolerance.
3.1 The Five-Phase Lifecycle
The operation of the protocol follows a distinct five-phase lifecycle, distinguishing it from the simple "prepare and measure" logic of BB84.
Phase 1: Preparation (The Input)
Alice (the transmitter) generates a random stream of bits. For each transmission, she randomly selects one of the four tetrahedral states |\psi_k\rangle (k \in \{0,1,2,3\}).
* No Basis Choice: Unlike BB84, Alice does not choose between an X-basis and a Z-basis. She simply chooses one of the four states from the single tetrahedral set.
* Encoding: The state is encoded onto the polarization of a photon (or a weak coherent pulse).
Phase 2: The Channel (The Environment)
The photon travels to Bob. During transit, it is subject to the environment:
* Unitary Evolution (U): Rotation of the polarization frame due to fiber birefringence.
* Depolarization: Loss of purity due to scattering.
* Eavesdropping: An adversary (Eve) attempting to measure the state. Because the four states are non-orthogonal, Eve cannot measure them without introducing a disturbance that deforms the ensemble.
Phase 3: Measurement (The Filter)
Bob (the receiver) measures the incoming photon using his SIC-POVM apparatus.
* Dual Frame Geometry: Bob’s measurement operators \{M_k\} are geometrically the "dual" of Alice’s signal states. In the Bloch sphere representation, the measurement outcomes are anti-aligned with the signal states.
* The Exclusion Principle: If Alice sends state |\psi_j\rangle, and Bob has a measurement outcome aligned with -\vec{r}_j, the probability of obtaining the outcome corresponding to |\psi_j\rangle is zero (in a noiseless system).
* Implication: A click at detector k tells Bob with certainty: "Alice did not send the state orthogonal to this detector."
Phase 3: Sifting (The Anti-Correlation Loop)
This is where the protocol diverges radically from classical QKD. Sifting is not about matching bases, but about elimination.
1. Bob's Announcement: Suppose Bob detects a photon at Detector 1. He knows Alice did not send State \text{Dual}(1). He effectively eliminates one possibility. To sift, Bob randomly selects two other outcomes he did not get (say, 2 and 3) and announces them publicly: "I did not get outcome 2 or 3."
2. Alice's Logic: Alice knows she sent State 0.
   * If Alice's state was one of the ones Bob announced (e.g., if she sent State 2), she announces "Failure." (This happens 2/3 of the time).
   * If Alice's state was not one of the ones Bob announced (she sent State 0, he announced "Not 2, Not 3"), she knows Bob's actual measurement must be consistent with her state.
3. Key Generation: Through this elimination game (similar to the logic of the "Six-State Protocol"), they distill a shared bit.
4. Intrinsic Error Checking: In BB84, users must sacrifice a portion of the key to estimate the Bit Error Rate (BER). In the Tetrahedron Protocol, the sifting rate itself reveals the error rate. If Eve is intercepting, she disturbs the anti-correlations. Bob will start getting "impossible" outcomes. This will cause the sifting success rate to deviate from the theoretical 1/3, alerting Alice and Bob immediately without sacrificing key bits.
3.2 Security Analysis and Noise Tolerance
The security of the Tetrahedron Protocol is derived from the difficulty of distinguishing non-orthogonal states.
* Intercept-Resend Attack: If Eve intercepts the photon, she must measure it. Since she doesn't know which of the four states it is, she must guess a basis. Her measurement projects the state, destroying the tetrahedral symmetry. When she resends a state to Bob, the fidelity of the anti-correlations drops.
* Geometric Alarm: Bob’s SIC-POVM reconstruction of \rho will show the tetrahedron "squashed" along the axis of Eve’s measurement.
* Noise Threshold: Analysis by Renes shows the protocol can tolerate a Bit Error Rate (BER) of up to 26.7% under standard intercept-resend attacks, superior to the 25% of BB84 and 22.7% of the Six-State protocol.
* Finite-Key Security: Because the protocol uses every detection event (either for key or for error estimation via sifting), it accumulates statistical significance faster than protocols that discard half the data due to basis mismatch. This provides better security for finite-length keys.
