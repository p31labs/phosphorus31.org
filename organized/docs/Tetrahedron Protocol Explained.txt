3.1 The Flaw of Orthogonality: Why BB84 Fails
Classical QKD protocols, such as the seminal BB84 (Bennett & Brassard, 1984), rely on Orthogonality. In these systems, Alice and Bob measure qubits along the Z-axis (North/South) or the X-axis (East/West). While revolutionary, this approach is "geometrically coarse".
1. Blind Spots: By measuring along orthogonal axes, the system effectively checks the "front door" and the "back door" but leaves the "windows" unguarded. Vast swathes of the Hilbert space remain unmonitored.
2. Destructive Sifting: If Alice prepares a state in the Z-basis and Bob measures in the X-basis, the result is random noise. This data must be discarded ("sifted"), resulting in a 50% loss of signal efficiency.
3. Epistemological Limit: BB84 is a "tripwire." It can detect that an eavesdropper is present (via Bit Error Rate), but it cannot describe what the eavesdropper is doing. It lacks tomographic completeness.
Johnson argues that the legal system operates like a BB84 protocol: it asks binary questions (Guilty/Not Guilty) that collapse the complex superposition of the family reality into a flat, low-fidelity outcome, destroying the nuance of the system and sifting out the "truth" of the relationships.
