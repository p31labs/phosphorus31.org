2.1 The Failure of Classical Neuroscience: The Binding Problem
For decades, the dominant paradigm in neuroscience has modeled neural firing as a Poisson process. In this view, the generation of an action potential (spike) is a stochastic, memoryless event governed solely by the summation of synaptic inputs and electrochemical gradients. The probability of a neuron firing in a given time window $\Delta t$ is expressed as $P_{classical} = \lambda(t) \Delta t$, where $\lambda(t)$ is the instantaneous firing rate. While this model adequately explains simple reflex arcs and low-level sensory transduction, it fails catastrophically when applied to higher-order cognition.1
The most glaring failure of the classical model is the Binding Problem. The human conscious experience is unified; we do not perceive color, motion, and shape as separate data streams, but as a single, cohesive object (e.g., "a red ball rolling"). However, the neural circuitry processing these attributes is widely distributed across the cortex (V4 for color, V5 for motion, IT for shape). The conduction delays for electrical signals traveling between these regions via axons range from 10 to 100 milliseconds.
If the brain relied solely on classical synaptic transmission, the synchronization required to bind these disparate inputs into a unified percept would be thermodynamically impossible within the observed timeframe of perception. The brain simply cannot "talk to itself" fast enough via chemical synapses to generate the unity of consciousness. The classical model predicts a fragmented, disjointed experience, yet we experience a seamless whole. This discrepancy suggests the existence of a mechanism for information transfer that transcends classical axonal limits—a mechanism that is effectively instantaneous and non-local.1
