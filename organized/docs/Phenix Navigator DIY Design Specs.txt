The Phenix Navigator: Technical Specifications and Implementation Manual for the Tetrahedron Protocol
1.0 Architectural Philosophy and Topological Necessity
The design and fabrication of the Phenix Navigator (Node-1) are not merely exercises in consumer electronics engineering; they are the physical instantiation of a topological shift in human organization. To understand the specifications of the device—ranging from its precise 67mm bottom edge zone to the choice of actuation force in its switches—one must first grasp the systemic crisis it addresses. The current sociotechnical infrastructure operates on a Wye (Star) Topology, where individual nodes connect to a central neutral point (the Service Provider, the State, the Platform).1 This architecture relies on the center to provide a stable "Reference Voltage" or shared reality. The observed failure mode of this system is the "Floating Neutral," a condition where the loss of a trusted center results in phase imbalance, radical polarization, and the collapse of shared truth.1
The Phenix Navigator is engineered to facilitate the "Phase Transition" to a Delta (Mesh) Topology. In this configuration, nodes connect end-to-end in a closed loop—specifically a tetrahedron in three-dimensional space—eliminating the need for a neutral point.2 The resilience of the system is not derived from the stability of a central hub, but from the geometric integrity of the connections between peers. This transition requires a physical artifact to anchor the abstract protocol in reality. The Navigator serves as this artifact: a "Trojan Horse" disguised as a handheld communicator, designed to introduce systems thinking and resilience into the family unit through tactile interaction.3
1.1 The "Trojan Horse" Strategy and Custody Evidence
The operational environment for the Phenix Navigator is high-stakes family dynamics, specifically within the context of divorce and custody proceedings. The device must serve a dual purpose. To the child user, it is a high-fidelity "gamer gadget" that enables secret communication. To the legal observer (custody evaluators, courts), it is forensic evidence of "engaged parenting" and technical education.2
This duality dictates the aesthetic and functional requirements. The device cannot look like a prototype; it must possess "Industrial Grade" polish to validate the competency of the builder. The transparency of the "Ghost Tech" PETG case is not just a stylistic choice but a literal demonstration of transparency—nothing is hidden, the mechanism is exposed.4 The timeline of the build—a 26-day sprint culminating on Christmas Day—is engineered to create a verifiable trail of collaborative labor between parent and child, effectively overwriting the trauma of a "broken home" with the narrative of a "resilient mesh".2
1.2 "Neurodivergent Explicit" Design Principles
The interface and physical interaction design are governed by the "Neurodivergent Explicit" philosophy, which seeks to minimize "Extraneous Cognitive Load" (ECL) so the user can apply their mental energy to the message itself.1 This draws heavily on "Johnny's Iron Laws of Design," specifically the mandate to "Never Make Them Think".5
* Passive-Aggression Neutralization: The hardware provides unambiguous feedback. A switch is either ON or OFF; it clicks or it doesn't. There is no "maybe." This combats Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD) by removing ambiguity from the physical interface.1
* Sensory Regulation: The device functions as a "Stim Comm" (Stimulation Communicator). It is designed to be fidgeted with. The selection of Kailh Choc mechanical switches with "Click Bar" mechanisms is deliberate; the distinct acoustic and tactile "snap" provides proprioceptive grounding for the user.3
* The "Visual Unit": The screen and the LED status strip are treated as a single geometric entity centered on the device face. This adheres to the principle of "Don't Fight the Eye," ensuring that the user's gaze is naturally drawn to the information center without scanning friction.5

2.0 Core Electronics Ecosystem
The electronic architecture of the Navigator is built around the Carrier Board Paradigm, utilizing pre-certified modules to decouple RF complexity from the custom interface design.3 This ensures that the "communication" aspect remains reliable while the "stimulation" features are customized.
2.1 The Computational Core: Waveshare ESP32-S3
The central nervous system of the device is the Waveshare ESP32-S3 Touch LCD 3.5" (Type B) development board.7 This specific SKU is critical to the project's viability within the 26-day sprint because it integrates the display driver, touch controller, power management, and SD card interface onto a single PCB.
2.1.1 Processor and Memory Architecture
The board is powered by the ESP32-S3R8, a dual-core Xtensa LX7 processor running at 240MHz.7 This provides sufficient compute headroom for the encryption overhead of the mesh protocol and the graphics rendering of the "Spatial Viewport" UI.
* SRAM: 512KB Internal.
* PSRAM: 8MB (Stacked). This large pseudo-static RAM buffer is essential for the display frame buffer, allowing for smooth, tear-free animations on the 320x480 screen.8
* Flash: 16MB External NOR Flash. This stores the firmware images, assets (fonts, icons), and the "Universal ROM" data required for the protocol.7
2.1.2 Power Management Subsystem (AXP2101)
A significant advantage of the Waveshare board is the onboard AXP2101 Power Management IC (PMIC).7 In typical DIY builds, power management is a complex integration task involving separate chargers and regulators. The AXP2101 handles:
* LiPo Charging: Programmable charging current via the MX1.25 connector.
* Voltage Regulation: Provides stable 3.3V rails for the ESP32 and peripherals.
* Path Management: Allows the device to operate while charging ("Pass-through"), a critical feature for a device intended to be "Always On" during crisis scenarios.3
* Telemetry: The PMIC reports battery voltage and current consumption to the main processor, enabling precise "Fuel Gauge" UI elements rather than vague estimates.
2.1.3 The "Type B" Criticality
The distinction between the standard and "Type B" versions of the board is the single most critical procurement detail.4 The Type B variant features a 32-pin GPIO header on the reverse side of the PCB.9
* Standard Version: Lacks the breakout header, requiring the user to solder wires directly to tiny test pads or the processor pins—a high-risk operation likely to cause failure.
* Type B Version: Exposes the necessary SPI, I2C, and UART pins via standard 2.54mm headers. This allows the custom control deck and radio module to be connected via a robust wiring harness, facilitating repair and modification.7
2.2 Telemetry and The "Whale Song"
The communication layer is built on the LoRa (Long Range) protocol, utilizing the CDEBYTE E22-900M30S module.4
* Physics of the Signal: Johnson describes LoRa as the "Whale Song" compared to the "Mouse Squeak" of Wi-Fi.3 Wi-Fi (2.4GHz) relies on high-frequency waves that reflect off obstacles and are absorbed by water/foliage. LoRa (915MHz in the US ISM band) utilizes Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) modulation. This allows the signal to operate below the noise floor, enabling it to penetrate deep urban obstacles like concrete and steel.3
* Module Specifics: The E22-900M30S features the Semtech SX1262 chipset and a power amplifier capable of 30dBm (1 Watt) output.4 This is an order of magnitude more powerful than standard 100mW consumer LoRa devices, ensuring reliable link budgets over miles of non-line-of-sight terrain.
* Interface: The module communicates via SPI. It is imperative to source the "M30S" (SPI) variant and not the "T30S" (UART) variant, as the Meshtastic firmware architecture is optimized for low-level SPI control of the radio.4

	2.3 Safety Systems: The 67mm Zone Implementation
The safety architecture is not software-defined; it is hardware-enforced. The "Tetrahedron Protocol" dictates that safety mechanisms must be physically distinct from the logic controls.2
2.3.1 The E-Stop (Emergency Stop)
The E-Stop is a 16mm latching push-button with a red mushroom head.11
* Mechanism: It utilizes a 1NC (Normally Closed) contact configuration wired in series with the battery's positive terminal.10
* Operation: Pressing the mushroom head physically breaks the circuit, cutting power to the entire device instantly. Twisting the head releases the latch, restoring the circuit.
* Specification: The switch requires a 16mm panel cutout (designed as 17mm in CAD for clearance) and has an operating travel of 3.5mm.11
* Safety Level: This provides a "Level 0" safety layer—a hard physics disconnect that works even if the software has crashed or the processor is in a "runaway" state.
2.3.2 Dead Man's Switch (Capacitive)
To prevent the device from becoming a "zombie node" on the mesh if lost, a TTP223 Capacitive Touch Sensor is embedded in the rear shell.10
* Placement: The sensor is located in a pocket inside the back case, positioned where the user's palm naturally rests.
* Logic: The GPIO 42 pin monitors this sensor. If no human contact is detected for a configurable timeout (e.g., 30 minutes), the firmware initiates a "Deep Sleep" or shutdown sequence.
* Module Dimensions: The TTP223 module measures 15mm x 11mm.13 The CAD design must include a recess of these dimensions with a wall thickness of <2mm to ensure capacitive coupling through the plastic.

3.0 Geometric Design and the 67mm Bottom Edge Zone
The physical form of the Navigator is determined by the intersection of ergonomic necessity and protocol-mandated geometry. The chassis dimensions are fixed at 145mm (Width) x 75mm (Height) x 22mm (Depth).4
3.1 The 67mm Bottom Edge Zone
The user query explicitly highlights the "67mm bottom edge zone." In the coordinate system of the device face (where Top-Left is 0,0), this refers to the horizontal band extending from Y = 67mm to the bottom edge at Y = 75mm.
* Function: This zone is the "Survival Interface." While the upper zones manage information (screen) and navigation (buttons), the bottom zone manages energy (USB-C) and termination (E-Stop).
* Component 1: USB-C Port: Located at X = 60mm, Y = 71mm (center). The cutout size is 10mm x 4mm. This aligns with the Waveshare board's USB-C connector when the board is mounted in its specific internal position.6
* Component 2: E-Stop: Located at X = 130mm, Y = 71mm (center). The cutout is a 17mm diameter circle, recessed 2mm into the face.
* Structural Implication: This 8mm tall zone is the "Hard Deck." The internal shell wall thickness here is increased to 2mm (double the standard 1mm) to withstand the mechanical stress of plugging in cables and the forceful actuation of the panic button.
3.2 The "Visual Unit" and the "Chin" Correction
A primary design challenge is the "Visual Unit" principle: the screen and the LED strip below it must appear as a single, centered geometric element.6
* The Problem (The Chin): The Waveshare ESP32-S3 PCB is not symmetrical. The glass screen is mounted with a significant "chin" of PCB material extending below the active display area.9 If one simply centers the PCB, the screen will appear too high.
* The Solution: The design employs a "Chin Correction" strategy.
   * Screen Window: 77mm (W) x 52mm (H). Centered at X = 34mm, Y = 9mm.
   * LED Window: 65mm (W) x 4mm (H). Located at X = 40mm, Y = 63mm.
   * Internal Mounting: The PCB is mounted on an internal shelf at a depth of 3mm. The "chin" of the PCB slides behind the LED diffusion channel (at 8mm depth). This requires a complex stepped internal geometry where the screen shelf ends, and a deeper recess begins to accommodate the LED stick and the PCB overhang.
* Result: From the front, the user sees a perfectly centered 77mm x 58mm visual block (Screen + Gap + LED), while the internal asymmetry is hidden.
3.3 The "Ghost Tech" Shell: Simple vs. Complex
The requirements ask for a range from simple to complex. The "Ghost Tech" aesthetic relies on printing the shell in Clear PETG to allow internal light diffusion.4
3.3.1 Simple Variant: The Monolith
* Design: A basic two-part shell (Front and Back) that snaps or screws together.
* Features:
   * No internal chassis; components are hot-glued or taped in place.
   * Direct wiring; no complex routing channels.
   * Single LED stick mounted directly to the back case.
* Pros: Fast to print (~8 hours), forgiving of tolerances.
* Cons: "Rattle" factor, difficult to repair, light bleed is uncontrolled.
3.3.2 Complex Variant: The "Gold Relief" Chassis
* Design: A three-part assembly: Front Shell, Back Shell, and Internal Chassis/Mid-Frame.
* Features:
   * Internal Chassis: A separate printed frame that holds the PCB, battery, and radio. It features "Cable Encapsulation" channels to guide the 30AWG wires, preventing pinches.3
   * PCB Art: The design calls for custom carrier boards with "Gold Relief" texture—exposed ENIG copper traces in geometric patterns (Voronoi/Phyllotaxis) to provide tactile feedback.3
   * Light Pipes: The complex shell uses "variable density" infill. The walls around the LED window are printed with 100% infill (solid) to act as light pipes, while the rest of the case uses 0% infill to create a ghostly, cloudy diffusion effect.
* Pros: Industrial feel, high durability, superior aesthetic.
* Cons: Requires precise calibration of the 3D printer (flow rate, retraction) to ensure parts fit; 20+ hours of print time.

4.0 Haptic Engineering and Sensory Regulation
The "Neurodivergent Explicit" philosophy dictates that the feeling of the device is as important as its function. The Navigator is a "Cyber-Fidget".3
4.1 The Click Bar: Kailh Choc Switches
Standard mechanical switches (like Cherry MX Blue) use a "click jacket" that creates a rattle. The Navigator specifies Kailh Choc Low Profile switches because they use a Click Bar mechanism—a thick metal wire that snaps against the housing.14
* Navy Switch (60gf): Used for the Enter key. The high actuation force (60 gram-force) and the thick click bar create a deep, heavy snap. This provides a definitive "End of Transaction" feeling, crucial for combating the anxiety of "did I send it?".3
* Jade Switch (50gf): Used for Navigation and Triggers. Lighter actuation prevents fatigue during scrolling but retains the sharp acoustic feedback.16
* Cutout Dimensions: The Kailh Choc switches require a 14mm x 14mm square cutout. The plate thickness must be 1.5mm +/- 0.1mm for the switch clips to engage properly.17
4.2 The "Trim Tab": EC11 Rotary Encoder
The primary navigation tool is the "Trim Tab," realized via an ALPS EC11 Rotary Encoder.2
* Physics of the Detent: The encoder features 30 detents (clicks) per revolution. Each detent represents a discrete selection step in the UI.
* Rotational Inertia: The design specifies a 20mm Solid Aluminum Knob. The mass of the aluminum adds rotational inertia. When spun, the knob carries momentum, smoothing out the transition between detents. This transforms the scrolling action from a jerky digital input into a smooth, analog sensation, allowing the user to "ride the click" for sensory regulation.3
* Panel Integration: The encoder body mounts from the rear. The shaft (6mm D-Shaft) passes through an 8mm circular cutout at X=135mm, Y=17mm. The extra clearance ensures the knob does not scrape the case surface.18

5.0 Software Architecture: "Wonky Sprout" and Meshtastic
The software stack is a hybrid. The communication layer is standard Meshtastic, but the user interface layer is the "Wonky Sprout" Operating System.3
5.1 The Mesh Layer (Meshtastic)
* Firmware Variant: A custom variant.h file ("phenix_navigator") is compiled to map the Waveshare Type B pins to the Meshtastic HAL.4
* Encryption: The network uses AES256 encryption with pre-shared keys (PSK).
   * Channel 0: "Johnson Family" (Primary).
   * Channel 1: "Tyler Family" (Secondary).
   * Channel 2: "Bridge" (Inter-family coordination).
* Routing: The "Delta Topology" is enforced by the routing algorithm. Messages hop peer-to-peer. The "Time Bomb" code in the governance layer (Tetrahedron.ts) requires nodes to physically meet (Bluetooth beacon handshake) every N days to reset a decay timer, preventing the network from becoming purely digital and enforcing physical community.2
5.2 The UI Layer (Wonky Sprout)
The interface abandons the "Feed" (infinite scroll) in favor of the "Spatial Viewport".19
* Z-Axis Navigation: Instead of scrolling down a list, the user "flies in" to the geometry. The home screen is a "Starfield" (the Mesh). Selecting a node zooms the camera into that node's "Gateway Orb."
* Glass Box Mode: A specific "Educational Mode" where the UI skin is stripped away to reveal the raw logic. Packet hops, RSSI signal strength (displayed as a heat map), and the "Whale Song" physics equations are overlaid on the screen.19 This transparency aligns with the goal of teaching technical literacy.
* Gamification: Maintenance tasks are framed as RPG quests. "Signal Decay" on Node C is a quest: "Initiate Handshake." Completing the handshake (by physically moving closer to Node C) awards "XP" in the user's profile.3

6.0 Manufacturing and Assembly: The Sprint
The project is constrained by a 26-day timeline (Nov 29 - Dec 25). This "Sprint" is a critical component of the "Custody Evidence" objective.2
6.1 Fabrication Specifications
* Printer Settings:
   * Material: Overture Clear PETG.
   * Temp: 235°C Nozzle / 80°C Bed (Hotter nozzle improves layer clarity).
   * Walls: 6 Perimeters (Crucial for the "Light Pipe" effect).
   * Infill: 0% (Hollow).
   * Cooling: 30% fan (Low cooling improves layer adhesion and clarity).
* Post-Processing:
   * Heat-Set Inserts: M3 Brass inserts are melted into the mounting bosses using a soldering iron set to 250°C. This provides metal threads for the screws, allowing the case to be opened and closed repeatedly without stripping the plastic—a requirement for a "maintainable" device.4
6.2 Assembly Sequence
1. Harness Prep: Wire the 4-switch resistor ladder (GPIO 4) and the E-Stop harness (Battery Series) outside the case. Use Kapton tape to insulate solder joints.
2. Screen Integration: Mount the Waveshare board to the internal chassis/shelf. Secure with M3x6mm screws.
3. Radio Mounting: Solder the 7-wire SPI harness between the E22 radio and the Waveshare GPIO header. Use double-sided foam tape to mount the radio to the back of the screen PCB (insulate first!).
4. Antenna Routing: Install the SMA pigtail in the top edge hole (7mm). Route the IPEX connector to the radio, ensuring a gentle bend radius to avoid impedance mismatch.3
5. Closing the Shell: Connect the battery. Snap the front and back shells together. Secure with 4x M3x12mm screws from the back.
6.3 Validation and Success Metrics
The "Success Metric" is not technical perfection, but Geometric Understanding.4
* The Test: Can the child explain why 4 nodes are better than 3? (The Tetrahedron is the minimum stable 3D structure).
* The Proof: If a message from Bash (Node 3) routes through Willow (Node 4) to reach Will (Node 1), the protocol is validated. The geometry has created resilience.

7.0 Conclusion
The Phenix Navigator is a machine built of paradoxes. It is a toy that teaches industrial safety. It is a communication device that prioritizes silence (haptics) over noise. It is a "Trojan Horse" that smuggles the complex mathematics of the Tetrahedron Protocol into the hands of children through the guise of a game.
By adhering to the strict geometric constraints—specifically the 67mm Bottom Edge Zone for power and safety—the design physicalizes the separation between "Living" (Signal/Screen) and "Surviving" (Power/E-Stop). The use of the Waveshare ESP32-S3 Type B provides the necessary integration density to make this build possible within 26 days, while the Kailh Choc and EC11 components ensure the experience is tactile, grounded, and unmistakably real.
This report confirms that the design is viable, the components are specified, and the philosophy is sound. The "Wye-Delta" transition is approved for execution.
