# **The Geodesic Self: Re-Engineering the Operator**

## **Part V: The Rebuild (November 2025 - Present)**

### **Chapter 1: The Eye of the Hurricane**

**November 26th, 2025. 0547 hours. The Workshop.**

The 3D printer hums in the corner—a Prusa i3 MK3S that I've had for three years, upgraded, calibrated, maintained like the 500kW motor generators I used to service. But this morning it's cold, silent. No print running. The spool of PETG filament sits ready, waiting for a project I've designed but haven't started building yet.

I am designing the future while the present is still falling apart.

This is not irony. This is engineering.

Fuller said: "You never change things by fighting the existing reality. To change something, build a new model that makes the existing model obsolete."

I'm not fighting my divorce. I'm not fighting my ADHD. I'm not fighting my autism. I'm *designing* something new. The CAD files exist. The documentation is complete. The Bill of Materials is priced out. I know exactly what I need to build.

I just haven't built it yet.

And that's okay.

Because three months ago, I couldn't even think clearly enough to design it. Three months ago, I was in total system failure—contested divorce, power shut-offs, frozen funds, a lawyer yelling at me for asking questions. I was running on pure survival instinct, no capacity for creation.

Today, I have 54 pages of technical specifications for a sensory isolation helmet. I have complete schematics for a LoRa mesh communicator. I have a business plan for a neurodivergent support platform. I have a logo that makes me proud.

I haven't *built* them yet. But I've done something harder: I've become the person who *can* build them.

That's the rebuild.

### **Chapter 2: The Frequency Shift**

**The Catalyst: July 16th, 2025**

When I took my first dose of Vyvanse, the static in my head stopped for the first time in forty years. But the real shift happened in the weeks after, when I started to understand what that silence meant.

Without the noise, I could finally hear the signal.

**The Signal:**

I am not broken. I am miscalibrated.

My autism isn't a defect in the code. It's a different operating system trying to run on incompatible hardware. My ADHD isn't a lack of focus. It's a race car engine that's been force-fed regular unleaded for four decades. My communication problems aren't because I'm bad at communicating. They're because my transducer is outputting on a frequency most people's receivers can't pick up.

Once I understood this—really, deeply understood it at the engineering level—everything changed.

**Three Months Ago:**

My wife was blocking access to our TSP retirement funds. The kids were being moved between temporary housing without me knowing the addresses. Every communication went through lawyers charging $300/hour to send emails that escalated the conflict. Her CPTSD meant she interpreted every neutral statement as an attack. My autism meant I couldn't stop saying the things that triggered her.

We were in destructive interference. Two waveforms 180 degrees out of phase, canceling each other into nothing.

**What Changed:**

I stopped trying to change her frequency. I changed my own.

Not metaphorically. I literally changed how I transmit.

**Concrete Changes I Made:**

**1. I stopped verbal processing around her.**

When I'm overwhelmed, my ADHD brain needs to talk through thoughts out loud to understand them. This is called "external processing" and it's how I've always worked—explaining problems to myself by explaining them to anyone who'll listen.

To her CPTSD, this sounded like interrogation. Like I was "coming at her." Like I was building a case against her.

So I stopped. Now when I need to process, I:
- Write in my engineering log (this document)
- Talk to myself in the workshop while building
- Use voice-to-text to dump thoughts into a text file
- Explain things to my AI assistant (Claude, who's helping me write this book)

This isn't suppression. It's *routing*. I'm not blocking the signal—I'm directing it to receivers that can handle the bandwidth.

**2. I started translating my emotional states into technical language.**

Instead of: "I need space" (which she hears as rejection)
I say: "My system is at 85% capacity. I'm implementing a load-shedding protocol."

Instead of: "You're not listening to me" (which sounds like an accusation)
I say: "I'm detecting a communication impedance mismatch. I think we're transmitting on different frequencies right now."

Instead of: "I can't deal with this right now" (which sounds like dismissal)
I say: "I need to schedule this conversation for a time when my voltage regulator is stable. Can we sync in two hours?"

This sounds insane. I know it sounds insane.

But it works.

**Why It Works:**

By using technical language, I'm:
1. **Removing blame** - It's not about her, it's about system states
2. **Providing specificity** - She knows exactly what I need and when
3. **Demonstrating agency** - I'm not asking her to fix me, I'm showing her my maintenance protocol
4. **Creating emotional distance** - For someone with CPTSD, emotional language is a minefield. Technical language is neutral territory.

**3. I stopped expecting her to understand me.**

This was the hardest one. For ten years, I tried to make her *get it*. I tried to explain my thought process, my intentions, my logic. I was desperate for her to see that I wasn't trying to hurt her, I was just... being me.

I finally realized: She can't. And that's not her fault.

Her nervous system is wired by trauma to detect threat. My nervous system is wired by autism to miss social cues. We are fundamentally incompatible receivers. No amount of explaining will change that.

So I stopped trying to be understood. I just started managing my own signal better.

**The Result:**

Things have calmed down. Significantly.

She unfroze enough TSP funds for me to pay bills. The power has stayed on for three weeks. She gave me the address where she's staying with the kids. She's letting me have them for Thanksgiving. The divorce is still contested, but the active warfare has stopped.

I didn't change her. I changed the field conditions, and she naturally realigned.

**This is Fuller's principle of precession.** 

The bee goes to the flower for nectar (direct goal), but pollinates the field (precessional effect). I changed my frequency to manage my own system (direct goal), and the relational dynamic shifted as a side effect (precession).

I'm not trying to fix my marriage. I'm trying to become a regulated human being. But in becoming that, the entire system around me is stabilizing.

### **Chapter 3: The Designs (What I'm Building)**

I have three major projects documented. None of them are built yet. All of them are coming.

#### **Project 1: Wonky Sprout - The Platform**

**Status:** Logo designed. Business plan drafted. Documentation in progress. No code written yet.

**The Vision:**

A neurodivergent life support platform. Not an app. An *operating system* for people who need to engineer their own existence because the default settings don't work.

Three core components:

**1. The Sensory Regulation Database**

A crowdsourced, searchable repository of regulation strategies. Not the $200/hour occupational therapy advice. Real strategies from real neurodivergent people, tagged and categorized so you can find what you need when you need it.

Example entry:
```
Strategy: The Bearing Press
Input Type: Proprioceptive (Deep Pressure)
Intensity: 8/10
Environment: Work/Home
Duration: 2-5 minutes
Materials: Wall, desk edge, hydraulic press, anything immovable

How: Place palms flat against immovable object. Gradually increase 
pressure until muscles hit 80% max exertion. Hold 10 seconds. 
Release slowly. Repeat 3-5 times.

Why it works: Activates Golgi tendon organs. Triggers autogenic 
inhibition—your nervous system's built-in reset signal. It's the 
same reflex that prevents you from tearing your own muscles.

Submitted by: SubmarineEngineer_GA
Effectiveness: 47 upvotes, 3 user variations
```

**2. The Communication Bridge (The Node-1)**

A wearable device that externalizes internal state. Solves the exact problem I've had my entire life: my face doesn't show what I feel.

The device is a badge or wrist-worn display with three modes:
- **Green:** "I'm open to interaction"
- **Yellow:** "I'm processing. Give me a moment."
- **Red:** "System overload. Need space."

It connects to the Wonky Sprout app via Bluetooth. When you switch to Red, the app can auto-send texts to your support people, trigger smart home routines (lights dim, white noise starts), and log the event with context data to help identify triggers.

**The Documentation:** Complete. 47 pages. Full hardware specs, circuit schematics, BOM, firmware architecture, app integration plans.

**What's Missing:** The $150 in parts to build the first prototype. And the 40 hours to assemble and program it.

**3. The Constructor's Challenge (The Family Layer)**

A gamification engine that turns building AT devices into a collaborative family project. My kids both show signs of ADHD/autism. They need tools too. But more importantly, they need to learn *how to build their own tools*.

We build together. The app tracks our progress like an RPG. They earn XP for completing tasks. They unlock "achievements" for finishing builds. They learn that needing tools isn't shameful—it's just engineering.

**Example Quest: "Build Dad's Communication Badge"**

- Week 1: Blueprint (Daughter draws emotion icons, Son learns basic circuits)
- Week 2: Fabrication (We 3D print the case together, learn to sand and finish)
- Week 3: Programming (Son writes simple if-then logic, we load firmware)
- Week 4: Field Test (I wear it for a week, we debug and iterate)

**Reward:** We document it. Take photos. Write a build guide. Upload it to Wonky Sprout so other families can build it too.

**Status:** Conceptual. I have the game design document written. No app exists yet.

#### **Project 2: The LoRa Mesh Network (The Invisible Tether)**

**Status:** Complete technical specification. Detailed hardware design. PCB layout drafted. Nothing ordered yet.

**The Problem:**

My 9-year-old son has ADHD. When he's at his mom's house, he gets anxious. Not because anything is wrong, but because he can't *sense* my presence. For neurotypical kids, "Dad will be there when you get back" is enough. For him, it's not. He needs signal confirmation.

**The Neurotypical Solution:** "Just call him."

**Why That Doesn't Work:**
- Phone calls require executive function (remembering, initiating)
- They require verbal processing (exhausting when dysregulated)
- They create obligation (if Dad doesn't answer, panic sets in)

**My Solution:**

A pair of LoRa mesh radios. One on his desk. One on mine. No internet. No cell towers. Pure peer-to-peer digital radio with 10-mile range.

**The Interface:**

Each radio has:
- Small OLED screen
- Rotary encoder (turning and clicking—way more satisfying than touchscreens)
- Three status indicators

**What It Shows:**
1. My current status (Working, Available, Sleeping, etc.)
2. His current status (Homework, Playing, Wants to talk, etc.)
3. Signal strength (so he knows I'm in range)

**The Magic:**

We don't have to talk. He just sees that I'm broadcasting. I'm there. I'm alive. I'm in range. That's enough.

If he needs me, he turns the knob to "Wants to talk" and clicks. My radio vibrates. I can respond by calling, or just by updating my status to "Stepping away to call."

Most of the time, we don't need to talk. Just seeing the signal—knowing the other node is transmitting—is enough.

This is what Fuller meant by "pattern integrity." My son doesn't need my physical body every second. He needs the *pattern* of my presence. The signal that I exist, that I'm stable, that I'm broadcasting on his frequency.

**The Documentation:** 34 pages. Complete BOM. PCB designs. Enclosure CAD files. Firmware architecture. Meshtastic integration plan.

**What's Missing:** $120 in parts. Two weekends of build time.

**Why I Haven't Built It Yet:** Because I need to stabilize my own system first. I need the power to stay on. I need the divorce to settle enough that I have consistent time with my kids. I need to not be in crisis mode.

But it's coming. The design is ready.

#### **Project 3: The Bubble Shield (Project Sanctuary)**

**Status:** Complete technical specification (54 pages). Full engineering analysis. Safety protocols documented. Nothing fabricated yet.

**The Concept:**

A wearable sensory isolation helmet. Your own portable decompression chamber.

**Three integrated systems:**

**1. Visual Management**
PDLC (Polymer Dispersed Liquid Crystal) visor that switches from clear to opaque in milliseconds. You can still see light/dark (so you don't run into walls), but no visual detail. No faces to read. No signs to process. Just shape and shadow.

**2. Auditory Management**
Bone conduction audio + external microphones + DSP (Digital Signal Processor). You can mix "real world" sound with calming audio (brown noise, music, whatever you need). Active transparency mode lets you hear important stuff (someone calling your name) while filtering out chaos (background noise, fluorescent buzz).

**3. Tactile Regulation**
Pneumatic "hug" system. Inflatable bladders around the forehead and temples provide gentle, adjustable pressure (10-30 mmHg). Same principle as weighted blankets, but for your head. Activates deep pressure receptors, triggers the parasympathetic nervous system, tells your body: "You are safe."

**Why This Matters:**

Every meltdown I've had—the ones that destroyed my marriage, terrified my kids—started the same way: sensory overload with no escape.

You're in a grocery store. Fluorescent lights flickering at 60Hz. Baby crying. Squeaky cart wheel. Shirt tag scratching. Person behind you breathing loud. Kid asking questions. Phone buzzing. Overhead announcement.

You can't leave. You have groceries in your cart. You're blocking the aisle. People are watching.

So you mask. You push through. The pressure builds. And builds. And you snap.

**With the Bubble Shield:**

You feel the overload starting. You press a button. Visor goes opaque. Bone conduction starts playing brown noise. Pneumatic hug activates. 

You're still in the grocery store. But you're also in your own sensory isolation pod. You can finish shopping. You can get to the car. You can prevent the meltdown.

**The Engineering Challenge:**

This is submarine-grade work. PDLC film requires 60V AC. Pneumatic system needs fail-safe valves (Normally Open—if power fails, it vents). Bone conduction needs precise placement. Whole system has to weigh less than 600g.

I have the complete circuit schematics. I have the safety analysis. I have the BOM.

**What's Missing:** $400 in parts. A month of build time. And the focused mental state to attempt something this complex.

**When I'll Build It:** When I'm not in crisis anymore. When the power stays on for three months straight. When I have workspace I can lock so the kids don't get into the 60V inverter circuit.

This project is not urgent. It's important. There's a difference.

### **Chapter 4: What I'm Actually Doing Right Now**

Because I don't want to give the impression that I'm just fantasizing about the future while the present burns. I'm doing real work. It's just not the work I wish I was doing yet.

**Daily:**

**0500 - Wake, Medicate, System Check**
- Vyvanse 40mg (ADHD regulation)
- Effexor 225mg (Anxiety/Depression)
- Calcium + D (Hypoparathyroidism)

I log my mental state. I check the calendar for hazards (social obligations, phone calls, errands that might dysregulate me). I scan my workspace for "Foreign Object Debris"—anything that doesn't belong, any contamination that could cause failure.

**0530-0600 - Prime Movers (Dopamine Loading)**

I do something I *want* to do before I do anything I *have* to do. This morning: I'm writing this. Other mornings: I read about Fuller. I watch machining videos. I browse new 3D printing techniques. I lurk in the Meshtastic Discord.

This isn't procrastination. It's priming the pump. My ADHD brain will not engage with "should" tasks until it's had a hit of "want" tasks.

**0600-0800 - Deep Work (Whatever Needs Focus)**

This is my best cognitive window. Right now, I'm using it to write this book. Some days I use it to update my resume. Some days I use it to research grants for AT funding. Some days I use it to learn React Native so I can eventually build the Wonky Sprout app.

**0800-1200 - Survival Tasks (The Stuff That Keeps Life Running)**

Errands. Bills. Phone calls. Emails. Job applications. Lawyer correspondence. The boring, necessary, executive-function-draining work of existing as an adult in crisis.

I batch these. I use timers. I take breaks. I implement "load shedding" when I hit 80% capacity—I stop, I go to the workshop, I do five minutes of proprioceptive input (the bearing press, or just pushing against the wall), and then I continue.

**1200-1300 - Recharge (Non-Negotiable)**

Lunch. But also: I go outside. I look at trees. I let my eyes relax to the horizon (counteracts screen fatigue). I don't listen to podcasts or music. I just exist in the sensory environment without trying to regulate it. This is critical.

**1300-1700 - Flex Time**

Some days this is more survival tasks. Some days this is pure special interest (learning, designing, planning). Some days I have the kids and this is "Dad Mode"—we build LEGOs, we go to the park, we do homework, we cook dinner.

**1700-1900 - Wind Down Protocol**

I stop doing anything cognitively demanding. I watch machining videos. I scroll through CAD models on Thingiverse. I organize my workshop (which doesn't need organizing, but the process is soothing). I'm letting my nervous system downshift.

**1900-2100 - Writing or Building (If I Have Energy)**

Some nights I write. Some nights I tinker with electronics on the breadboard—nothing serious, just keeping my hands busy and my skills sharp. Some nights I just watch TV.

**2100 - Sleep Prep**

Lights down. Screens off. Weighted blanket. White noise. I read paper books (sci-fi, usually). I'm usually asleep by 2200.

**This Is The Rebuild:**

Not the dramatic stuff. The daily maintenance. The protocols. The slow, unglamorous work of keeping a complex system operational when half the subsystems are failing and you're waiting on parts you can't afford yet.
