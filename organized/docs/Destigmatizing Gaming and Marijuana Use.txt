Breaking the Stigma: A Functional Systems Analysis of "Maladaptive" Coping in Late-Diagnosed Neurodivergence
Executive Summary: From Pathology to Physics
The prevailing clinical and societal narrative surrounding substance use—specifically cannabis—and excessive digital engagement, such as video gaming, often relies on a framework of pathology. This traditional view frames these behaviors as "maladaptive," indicative of failures in character, discipline, or moral fortitude. However, when these behaviors are examined through the lens of late-diagnosed Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) and Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), particularly within the context of high-masking individuals who have navigated decades without support, they reveal themselves not as addictions in the pejorative sense, but as unauthorized engineering solutions to systemic biological problems.
This report, constructed from a rigorous synthesis of clinical literature, neurobiological research, and the personal "engineering" documentation of the subject, Will Johnson, aims to dismantle the stigma surrounding his use of video games and marijuana. By analyzing the subject's archival data—ranging from childhood behavioral reports to complex engineering protocols developed in adulthood—we can reframe these past behaviors as "Phase I: Chemical Damping" and "Digital Flow Control." These were not acts of rebellion or escapism, but functional accommodations unconsciously adopted to manage a "Noise Floor" that perpetually exceeded the safe operating limits of the subject's neurology.
The analysis demonstrates that the subject’s history, including early behavioral outliers like arson and legal involvement, was a manifestation of extreme emotional dysregulation and sensory gating deficits inherent to an unsupported neurotype. The transition to "Phase II: Structural Resilience," codified in the subject's "Tetrahedron Protocol," represents a cognitive pivot from suppressing symptoms to redesigning the environment—a philosophy termed "Human Gardening." This report provides a comprehensive breakdown of the mechanisms of action for these past coping strategies and offers an exhaustive, evidence-based roadmap for substituting them with sustainable, non-pharmacological alternatives that align with the "Artificer" and "Alchemist" skill trees defined in the subject's personal framework.

Part I: The Physics of the Neurodivergent Mind
To functionally analyze the utility of cannabis and gaming, one must first establish the "operating parameters" of the neurodivergent brain. The subject's personal documents frequently utilize engineering metaphors—"voltage," "noise floor," "governor droop"—to describe his internal state. These are not merely poetic comparisons; they are accurate, functional descriptors of the underlying neurophysiology of Autism and ADHD. By translating clinical symptoms into physics-based constraints, we can understand why "maladaptive" behaviors were, in fact, logical responses to a system running out of tolerance.
1.1 The "Noise Floor" and Sensory Gating Deficits
The concept of the "Noise Floor," as extensively described in the subject's "Investigating 'Tetra Hydro' Meanings" document 1, refers to the baseline level of background interference in a signal processing system. In high-fidelity audio engineering, for a signal to be clear, it must be significantly louder than this floor. In a neurotypical brain, a biological mechanism known as "sensory gating" functions as a noise reduction filter. It suppresses irrelevant stimuli—the hum of a refrigerator, the texture of a shirt tag, the flicker of a fluorescent light—allowing the brain to focus its limited processing power on the "signal" (the primary task or conversation).
Research consistently indicates that autistic individuals exhibit significant deficits in sensory gating. This is often quantified using the P50 suppression paradigm in electroencephalography (EEG) studies. In this test, a subject hears two identical auditory clicks played 500 milliseconds apart. A neurotypical brain, recognizing the redundancy, significantly suppresses its neural response to the second click. In contrast, the autistic brain often processes the second click with the same "high voltage" intensity as the first.2 The filter is broken. The brain fails to categorize the input as "redundant," treating every sensory packet as a novel, high-priority threat.
For the subject, Will Johnson, this means the "Noise Floor" is perpetually elevated. The "static roar" described in his documentation is the experiential correlate of this failure to inhibit redundant data. Every sensory input is processed with "high voltage" urgency, leading to a state of chronic hyper-arousal. Without a pharmacological or behavioral damper to manually lower this gain, the unmodulated input leads to "system rupture," clinically known as a meltdown or shutdown.1
The subject’s clinical history supports this "noise floor" hypothesis. Reports of "running away" as a child (getting no further than the driveway) and "tantrums when brothers moved his things" 3 can be reinterpreted not as emotional bratitutde, but as desperate attempts to control an environment that was assaulting his processing center with unfilterable noise. The "tantrum" is a system purge; the "running away" is an attempt to sever the connection to the noise source.
1.2 The Dopamine Deficit and the "Seeking" Engine
The co-occurrence of ADHD introduces a second, compounding variable: dopamine dysregulation. Dopamine is the neurotransmitter responsible for reward, motivation, and, crucially, the signal-to-noise ratio in neural circuits. It acts as the "tuning knob" that allows the brain to lock onto a specific frequency.
In the ADHD brain, low tonic dopamine levels result in a "seeking" state. The brain is chronically under-stimulated, unable to lock onto relevant tasks because the "signal" is not chemically amplified enough to rise above the "noise." This results in the "hunting" or "motoring" behavior described in the subject's engineering logs—a restless search for high-stimulation inputs to artificially boost dopamine levels.
This creates a paradoxical state for the AuDHD (Autistic + ADHD) individual:
1. Autism: The sensory gain is too high (Input is painful).
2. ADHD: The internal stimulation is too low (Boredom is painful).
The subject is trapped between the need for less sensory noise and the need for more dopaminergic stimulation. This specific push-pull dynamic creates a "high-voltage" state where the engine is revving (ADHD seeking) but the transmission is slipping (Autistic overwhelm).
1.3 Neuroinflammation: The "Hot" Brain
The subject frequently describes feeling "hot" or running at "high voltage" under stress. This aligns with emerging neuroimmunology research suggesting that ASD is characterized by a baseline state of chronic neuroinflammation. Stress triggers the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (such as IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-12).1
In a neurotypical system, this inflammatory response is transient. In the autistic system, due to the constant "threat" of sensory overload, the inflammatory cascade can become chronic. The sensation of "running hot" described by the subject is likely a literal interoceptive perception of this systemic inflammation. The "conduct issues" of the subject's youth—specifically the act of setting a 10-acre grass field on fire at age 6 3—can be reinterpreted through this lens. It was likely an impulsive, externalized attempt to discharge this unbearable internal tension. The fire outside mirrored the fire inside. It was a manifestation of "extreme emotional dysregulation" driven by a lack of inhibitory control and a desperate need to dump "thermal" energy.3
1.4 The "Transducer Error": Social Friction
A critical component of the subject's stress load is what he terms "Transducer Errors." In engineering, a transducer converts energy from one form to another (e.g., a microphone converts sound waves to electrical signals). If the transducer is faulty, the output signal does not match the input intent.
The subject reports that he is "misrepresented a lot" because others cannot accurately read his internal state from his nonverbal cues.3 His wife notes that his facial expressions do not match his feelings.3 This creates a feedback loop of social friction:
1. Input: Subject feels focused or neutral.
2. Transducer Error: Face displays anger or detachment ("Resting Bitch Face").
3. Output: Receiver (wife/peer) perceives hostility and reacts defensively.
4. Feedback: Subject is confused and hurt by the defensive reaction, increasing his "Noise Floor" (anxiety).
This pervasive misalignment forces the subject to manually "operate" his face—consciously manipulating eye contact and expressions—which is a high-energy "masking" task that rapidly depletes his "spoons" (energy reserves).3

Part II: Phase I Analysis – Chemical Damping (The Functional Role of Cannabis)
With the "operating parameters" defined—high noise floor, low dopamine, chronic inflammation, and transducer errors—we can now analyze the subject's use of cannabis. In his personal documentation, this is categorized as "Phase I: Chemical Damping".1 This terminology is precise and reframes the behavior from a moral failing to a mechanical necessity.
2.1 The Endocannabinoid System (ECS) as a Governor
The Endocannabinoid System (ECS) is the body's "master regulator" of homeostasis. It influences mood, appetite, pain perception, and, most importantly for the autistic brain, synaptic plasticity and neuronal excitability. Research suggests that ASD is linked to inherent dysregulation in the ECS, potentially involving altered signaling of anandamide, the body's natural "bliss molecule" (an endogenous cannabinoid).1
The subject’s query regarding "Tetra Hydro" points directly to the molecule $\Delta^9$-tetrahydrocannabinol (THC). THC acts as a partial agonist at CB1 receptors, which are densely populated in the brain's central nervous system, specifically on presynaptic neurons in the glutamate and GABA circuits.
2.2 Mechanism of Action: The Glumate Brake
One of the leading theories of autistic neurophysiology is the Excitatory/Inhibitory (E/I) Imbalance. The autistic brain tends to have an excess of Glutamate (the primary excitatory neurotransmitter) and a deficit of GABA (the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter). This creates a system prone to "runaway excitation"—the neural equivalent of a microphone feedback loop.
* Presynaptic Inhibition: When THC binds to CB1 receptors on presynaptic glutamate neurons, it inhibits the release of glutamate.
* The Result: It acts as a chemical "brake" on the runaway neural firing that characterizes sensory overload.1
By reducing the rate of synaptic firing, THC effectively increased the "viscosity" of the subject's mental environment. It artificially lowered the "Noise Floor," allowing the processor to catch up with the data stream. This explains the subject's report that cannabis allowed him to "engage or escape reality and moderate intense emotions".3 It was a crude but effective way to manually adjust the gain on his sensory inputs, damping the "static roar" to a manageable hum.
2.3 Immunological "Cooling"
The subject’s observation of "cooling" the system also has a pharmacological basis. While CB1 receptors are primarily neural, CB2 receptors are found primarily on immune cells and modulate inflammation.
* Cytokine Suppression: Research demonstrates that THC and other cannabinoids can inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines like Interferon-gamma (IFN-$\gamma$) and Interleukin-12 (IL-12).1
* Systemic Effect: By ingesting cannabis, the subject was likely self-medicating a chronic neuroinflammatory state. He was physically lowering the "temperature" of his nervous system, countering the "high voltage" sensation of stress-induced inflammation.
2.4 The "Black Band" of Safety
The subject adapted the term "Black Band Calibration" from his work at the Trident Refit Facility. In generator testing, the "black band" is a commutation zone where a generator runs without sparking—a safety margin.
* The Mechanism: THC provided a chemically induced "wide black band." It created a buffer zone where emotional shocks or sensory spikes could be absorbed without causing a "spark" (meltdown) or "frequency collapse" (shutdown).1
* The Trap: While effective as a "patch," this method created a dependency. The structural integrity of the system relied on an external chemical agent. When the agent was removed, or tolerance built up, the "black band" narrowed, leaving the subject vulnerable to the raw volatility of his unbuffered neurotype. This led to the "Symptom-Based Misdiagnosis Loop" described in the diagnosis documents, where his substance use was labeled as "addiction" rather than "unsupported autism".1

Part III: Digital Flow Control (The Functional Role of Video Games)
Just as cannabis provided chemical damping, the subject's engagement with video games provided "Digital Flow Control"—a structured, predictable environment that offered relief from the chaos of the physical and social world.
3.1 Flow State and Monotropism
The subject's affinity for video games is best understood through the theory of Monotropism. Proposed by Dinah Murray, Wenn Lawson, and Mike Lesser, Monotropism posits that autistic minds are "interest systems" that devote vast amounts of attention to a small number of interests at any one time. This is described as an "attention tunnel".5
* The Flow Channel: Video games are engineered to induce Flow—a psychological state of optimal experience where challenge meets skill. For a monotropic mind, the "attention tunnel" of a game is a place of refuge. It is a "Monotropic Flow State" where the scattered resources of the brain align into a singular, powerful laser of focus.6
* Recovery vs. Addiction: While neurotypical observers might label long gaming sessions as "addiction" or "escapism," for the autistic brain, this state is restorative. It allows the sensory and social processing centers to go offline, providing a "rest and digest" period for the exhausted nervous system.7 The subject’s "repetitive behaviors" and "intense interests" in CAD and electronics 3 are manifestations of this same need for monotropic focus. The game provides a channel where the "current" of his attention can flow without resistance.
3.2 Polyvagal Safety and Predictability
Polyvagal Theory, developed by Dr. Stephen Porges, explains how the autonomic nervous system scans for safety. The neurodivergent nervous system often perceives the ambiguous, unpredictable social world as a threat, keeping the individual in a state of sympathetic mobilization (fight/flight).7
* Deterministic Systems: Video games are deterministic systems. Input A always equals Output B. The rules are rigid, visible, and consistent. This provides a sense of "geometric stability" that the real world lacks.
* The "Wye" Topology of Social Life: The subject contrasts the "Wye" (Star) configuration of the social world—which relies on unstable "neutral" points—with the "Delta" configuration of engineering. Games operate on a "Delta" logic: they are robust and predictable.
* The "Dad-Interface" Prototype: The subject’s development of the "Wonky Sprout" helmet 1—an external LED matrix to display emotions—mirrors the logic of video games. In games, status effects (health, mood) are explicitly displayed on a HUD (Heads-Up Display). The subject sought to bring this binary clarity into the analog world to bypass the "transducer errors" of his own facial expressions. He was attempting to "mod" real life to have the UI of a game.
3.3 Dopamine Regulation and Executive Scaffolding
For the ADHD brain, video games provide an externalized executive function system.
* Externalized Motivation: The ADHD brain struggles with "Reward Prediction Error"—it cannot "feel" the value of a future reward. Games provide immediate feedback loops (points, levels, loot) that trigger dopamine release, compensating for the ADHD brain's deficit. They "gamify" effort, providing the "Objective Quality Evidence" (OQE) that the subject craves.
* The "Artificer" Skill Tree: The subject’s own "Gamified Operating System" creates "skill trees" for real-life skills (e.g., "Artificer" for hardware repair). This is a direct translation of gaming mechanics into life management. It proves that the gaming mindset is not a liability but a powerful cognitive framework for the subject. He is not "addicted" to games; he is addicted to the clarity of progress that games provide.
