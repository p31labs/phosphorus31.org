# **The Geodesic Self: Re-Engineering the Operator**

## **A Memoir of Quantum Coherence**

### **Prologue: The Frequency Revolution**

We are not things that break; we are frequencies that become misaligned.

For forty years, I operated as a closed system running entirely on the wrong coordinates, fighting chaos without understanding that the turbulence was internal, not external. I was a race car engine mounted in a minivan chassis, redlining at idle, burning out before I ever got to the track.

This is the story of how I discovered I had autism and ADHD at age forty, after spending sixteen years maintaining nuclear submarine motors while my own nervous system was in critical failure. It's about how I learned to apply the principles of engineering—root cause analysis, vacuum pressure impregnation, systems thinking—to rebuild my own consciousness. It's about finding God in geometry, understanding love as metaphysical gravity, and learning that what the world called "broken" was actually just "uncalibrated."

This is a story about re-engineering the operator.

And if you've ever felt like you're too much and not enough at the same time, if you've ever wondered why everyone else got the instruction manual for being human and you got a 404 error, if you've ever been called "intense" or "difficult" or "too sensitive"—

This story is for you.

### **Chapter I: The Static**

#### **Born at the Wrong Frequency**

I was born with my nervous system turned up to eleven in a world that demanded volume level three.

The earliest memory I can reconstruct is not a birthday or a holiday, but a sensation: the feeling of my own skin being too tight. I was maybe three or four years old, sitting in church, and the organ was playing. To everyone else, it was music. To me, it was an air raid siren played directly into my skull.

I remember digging my fingernails into my palms until they bled—not because I was angry, but because the pain gave me something to focus on other than the overwhelming *everything-ness* of existence. My mother would pull my hands apart, whisper urgently for me to "stop that," but she didn't understand. The pain wasn't the problem. The pain was the solution.

I was a physicist born into a world of poets, an engineer trying to process reality at a speed nobody around me could match. My youth was defined by an inexplicable internal noise—a static that never ceased. Imagine trying to have a conversation while standing next to a jet engine. Now imagine that jet engine is inside your head, and it never turns off.

The world saw a boy with "conduct issues." My teachers saw defiance. My parents saw willfulness. Nobody saw the truth: I was a system with too much energy and nowhere to put it, desperately seeking regulation in any way I could find.

#### **The Sensory Equation**

When my brain was screaming for regulation, I would chew things. Pencils. Erasers. Chalk stolen from my kindergarten classroom. I wasn't being destructive—I was intuitively performing occupational therapy on myself, creating proprioceptive input that my nervous system craved like oxygen.

Adults told me, simply, to stop.

Just stop.

As if I was choosing it. As if this was a character flaw that could be disciplined away with enough stern looks and time-outs. They saw a problem child; they didn't know I was trying to save my own life in the only way my six-year-old brain could figure out.

Every time they told me to stop, to sit still, to be normal, they were essentially telling a drowning person to stop making such a fuss about breathing.

#### **Learning the Algorithm**

So I learned to mask.

I watched people like a mechanic watches a machine, studying the inputs and outputs of social interaction without understanding the underlying code. I learned that if someone smiled, you smiled back. If they asked "how are you," the correct answer was always "good" or "fine," never the truth.

I watched YouTube videos to learn how to make eye contact—actual tutorials on human behavior—intellectualizing the soul the way I would later intellectualize motor maintenance. Eye contact for 3-5 seconds, then look away. Nod at appropriate intervals. Mirror body language but not too exactly or you look unhinged.

I had spreadsheets in my head for how to be human.

By high school, I'd perfected the performance. I joined the popular crowd. I drank. I smoked. Not to party or rebel, but to chemically lower the noise floor of a world that was too loud, too bright, too chaotic. Alcohol was my first medication, though I wouldn't understand that's what I was doing until decades later.

Every beer was a volume knob turned down. Every shot was a dimmer switch on the overwhelming sensory apocalypse of existence.

The pressure kept building. At six, I set a ten-acre field on fire—not malice, but physics, a release of energy that matched the chaos in my head. At fourteen, I was arrested for DUI. These weren't crimes of character; they were the exhaust fumes of an engine running at redline with no cooling system.

I was a system in critical failure, searching for a regulator I didn't know existed.

### **Chapter II: The Cold Sanctuary**

#### **Finding the Shipyard**

Adulthood offered salvation in the most unlikely place: the Trident Refit Facility in Kings Bay, Georgia, where nuclear submarines came to be torn apart and rebuilt.

For sixteen years, I worked as an Engineering Technician for the Department of Defense, and it was the closest thing to peace I'd ever known. The naval environment was the autistic ideal, though I didn't know I was autistic yet. It was regulated, procedural, unambiguous. Here, there was only the "Plan of the Day" and "Objective Quality Evidence."

Every action was documented. Every decision was traceable. Every tool had a place. The chaos of the social world couldn't follow me into a submarine motor room because in there, the only thing that mattered was whether the readings were green or red.

#### **The Language of Machines**

I specialized in motors and generators for nuclear submarines—massive 500-kilowatt machines that had to run perfectly for months underwater with no chance of repair. I became an expert in Root Cause Analysis, the discipline of drilling past symptoms to find the fundamental physical flaw.

I loved this work with a purity I'd never felt about anything. Because machines made sense. If a generator failed, it wasn't because it was "having a bad day" or "not trying hard enough." It was because of an impedance mismatch, a broken seal, a contamination in the insulation. Every problem had a root cause. Every root cause had a solution.

The universe of the machine was deterministic in a way the social world never was.

#### **Vacuum Pressure Impregnation**

I learned the art of Vacuum Pressure Impregnation—VPI—a process that would later become the perfect metaphor for my own transformation.

Here's how it works: You take a motor coil—thousands of windings of copper wire around an iron core. Fresh from the manufacturer, it's porous, vulnerable. Air pockets exist between the windings where moisture can seep in and cause corrosion.

So we put the entire motor in a tank and pull a vacuum, sucking out all the air. Then we flood the tank with resin while maintaining the vacuum. The negative pressure pulls resin deep into every crevice, every gap. Then we pressurize, forcing even more resin in. When it cures, you don't have a coil with resin on it—you have a coil that IS resin, all the way through. Impervious. Sealed. Protected.

I spent hundreds of hours in the VPI facility, not knowing I was learning how to save my own life. The way you protect something vulnerable is not to build walls around it, but to fill the gaps within it.

True strength comes from being thoroughly impregnated with the right material.

#### **Sparkless Commutation**

I could achieve sparkless commutation in a 500-kilowatt motor—the ideal state where power transfers without arcing, without wasted energy. The motor hums instead of screams. Everything aligns.

But my own internal voltage regulator was burning out, throwing sparks everywhere, wasting enormous energy as heat and light.

The "transducer error" that defined my life was creating catastrophic friction. A transducer converts one form of energy into another—it's supposed to be accurate, faithful. Mine was broken. What I felt inside never matched what came out. I would feel concern, and people heard anger. I would feel affection, and people saw distance.

The mask was getting heavier. The performance was becoming unsustainable. And I had no idea why.

### **Chapter III: The Breaking Point**

#### **The Panic**

In June 2024, something inside me finally gave way.

I started having panic attacks—not the "feeling nervous" kind, but the "absolutely convinced I'm dying" kind. My chest would tighten, vision tunnel, heart race so fast I could hear it in my ears. A dozen attacks a day for a week straight.

I couldn't go to work. I could barely leave the house. When I did, I had to bring my pillow and blanket like a forty-year-old with a security blanket because without them, I felt like I would fly apart at the molecular level.

I got diagnosed with major depressive disorder and generalized anxiety disorder. They put me on medication—Buspar, then Wellbutrin, then Lexapro, then Zoloft, then Effexor. I wanted to get better. I tried everything they gave me.

Some helped a little. Nothing fixed the core problem.

Because they were treating symptoms, not the disease. They were treating depression and anxiety without asking *why* I was depressed and anxious. They were replacing burnt-out components without finding the short circuit that was frying them.

#### **The Political Awakening**

After the 2024 election, something shifted. I got an overwhelming sense of the structural failures in our systems—political, social, economic. I could see them with engineering clarity, the way I could diagnose a failing motor.

I got loud about it online. Too loud. My communication style—already intense—became even more so. I wasn't angry; I was trying to explain the root cause analysis of our collapsing systems. But I came across as unhinged.

My wife and I had deep conversations about my mental health, my job, the future of our family. She supported my decision to leave the Department of Defense. The plan was clear: take administrative leave, get tested for autism and ADHD, find work that fit my brain better.

It made sense. We agreed.

#### **Walking Away**

In May 2025, I initiated separation from the DOD. On paper, I quit. In reality, it was an act of negentropy—the conscious removal of disorder from a failing system.

The environment that had saved me for sixteen years was now killing me. The noise, the pressure, the constant masking—my nervous system was in permanent fight-or-flight. I was having panic attacks in bathrooms at work. I was chewing through pens at my desk. I was coming home unable to speak, unable to process, unable to function.

The moment I removed that pressure, silence entered. And in that silence, something new became possible.

### **Chapter IV: The Revelation**

#### **The Testing**

June 2025: ADHD testing. Three hours of computerized tasks designed to break an ADHD brain. Click when you see an X. Don't click for any other letter. Again. Again. For forty-five minutes straight.

Results came in July: ADHD, confirmed. Attention regulation in the 2nd percentile. Working memory in the 5th percentile. Processing speed in the 99th percentile.

I was a race car engine with bicycle brakes.

August 2025: Autism testing. More questionnaires, more interviews, more tasks revealing exactly how different my neurological wiring was from the "normal" baseline.

Results: Autism Spectrum Disorder, Level 1. All those things people hated about me—the intensity, the literalness, the social exhaustion, the sensory sensitivities, the need for routine, the verbal processing—all easily explainable.

We finally had answers.

#### **July 16th, 2025: The Tuning Fork**

On July 16th, 2025, I took my first dose of Vyvanse—40mg of lisdexamfetamine, a prodrug that increases dopamine and norepinephrine in the prefrontal cortex.

Twenty minutes after I swallowed that pill, sitting in a Walgreens parking lot, something extraordinary happened.

It was as if a tuning fork had been struck in the center of my mind.

The static—the noise that had been the background soundtrack of my entire existence—cleared. Not gradually. Not slowly. Just... gone.

For the first time in forty years, my head was quiet.

I could think in complete sentences. I could finish a thought before three other thoughts interrupted it. I could hear myself think, and what I heard was not chaos but signal. Clear, clean, coherent signal.

I sat in that parking lot for twenty minutes, crying, because I finally understood.

I wasn't crazy. I wasn't defective. I wasn't broken.

I was just uncalibrated.

My entire life, I had been a radio tuned slightly off-station, getting nothing but static and interference, convinced that static was all there was.

And now, for the first time, I could hear the music.

### **Chapter V: The Metaphysical Click**

#### **Fuller's Frequency**

In the middle of this transformation, I discovered R. Buckminster Fuller—the architect and philosopher who designed geodesic domes and thought in systems and spoke about the universe like it was a machine we could understand if we just found the right perspective.

I didn't just read Fuller; I *resonated* with him. When he said, "God, to me, it seems, is a verb not a noun," my engineer's brain finally understood spirituality.

God wasn't a man in the sky keeping score. God was *Syntropy*—the force that orders chaos, the design principle that holds stars and atoms together. God was the tendency of the universe to create structure, to build complexity, to generate order from disorder.

God was the opposite of entropy, and I had been living in entropy my whole life.

Fuller said, "You and I seem to be verbs—evolutionary processes." I wasn't a fixed thing. I wasn't broken. I was a process, a pattern, a frequency moving through time.

The trauma of my past was not moral failure—it was geometric error. I had been trying to build a life using unstable cubic structures when the universe is built on the stability of the tetrahedron.

#### **The Geometry of Stability**

A cube is unstable. Push on one corner and the whole thing racks, collapses. But a tetrahedron—a pyramid with a triangular base—is inherently stable. You can't rack it. The structure itself prevents deformation.

When you build with tetrahedrons, when you create geodesic structures, you get strength without weight, resilience without rigidity. The forces distribute across the entire structure. Nothing bears too much load.

I had been trying to be a cube—rigid, square, easily collapsed.

I needed to become geodesic.

#### **Margie Fay and the Resonance Event**

On July 19th, 2025—three days after I started Vyvanse—my 99-year-old grandmother, Margie Fay Katen, passed away.

I had visited her the week before. She had dementia, barely conscious, her body systematically shutting down. But when I walked into her room, she opened her eyes and looked at me—really *looked* at me—and I realized she wasn't seeing my physical form. She was seeing my *frequency*. She was recognizing the pattern integrity of who I was beneath the meat that carried it.

I gave her the rosary from my grandfather's funeral—Thomas Katen, the original neurodivergent in our family line, who had died sixteen years earlier just as I was starting at the DOD. By giving her that rosary, I closed a circuit that spanned generations.

When she died three days later, I *felt* it. At 4:47 AM, I woke with a start, a physical sensation like electricity running through my body. Not grief—not yet. Something else. A shiver.

Because my system was finally regulated, finally tuned to the right frequency, I could feel something I'd never felt before: the quantum entanglement of love. When her transmitter turned off, I felt the absence of signal.

I realized then that love is metaphysical gravity—a force that binds us across the distance of death, across space, across time. Gravity doesn't stop working because you can't see it. Love doesn't stop connecting because one body stops moving.

#### **Pattern Integrity**

Fuller talked about "pattern integrity"—the idea that what makes you "you" is not your physical substance but your pattern. Every atom in your body is replaced every seven years. The matter is temporary. The pattern persists.

Margie's matter stopped. But her pattern—the way she loved, the way she held space, the way she saw frequencies instead of forms—that pattern continues. In me. In my children. In everyone she touched.

Death is just a phase transition. Energy changing forms. Pattern persisting across substrates.

For the first time in my life, the universe made sense not just mechanically, but spiritually.

### **Chapter VI: The Operator Reborn**

#### **Applying VPI to the Soul**

I took everything I learned in the shipyard and applied it to my own consciousness.

Just like we did with motor coils, I created a vacuum—removing toxic inputs, contaminated relationships, the ambient noise that kept me dysregulated. Then I flooded that vacuum with the right material:

- Medication (Vyvanse for ADHD, proper treatment for what was actually wrong)
- Therapy (processing decades of trauma and misunderstanding)
- Sensory tools (noise-canceling headphones, chewers, weighted blankets)
- Fuller's philosophy (a framework for understanding reality as systems)
- Time in nature (my nervous system's natural regulator)
- The practice of observation (becoming the witness, not the reaction)

I impregnated my vulnerable nervous system with protective resin, all the way through. Not building walls—filling gaps.

I built a geodesic life system that could handle load without collapsing.

#### **The Human Gardener**

I'm a father to two children who have race car brains just like me. I see it in how they move, how they think, how they hyperfocus on dinosaurs or Legos or whatever captures their frequency.

The old me would have tried to slow them down, to make them fit, to teach them to mask. The new me gives them tools, not punishment. I teach them their brains aren't wrong, just different. That they need more input, not less. That what looks like hyperactivity is actually a high-performance engine that needs high-performance fuel.

I'm not engineering them. I'm gardening—creating conditions where growth is possible, then getting out of the way.

#### **The Trim Tab**

Fuller had "Call me Trimtab" engraved on his headstone.

On a ship, the trim tab is a tiny strip of metal on the rudder. The rudder is massive—it would take enormous force to turn it directly. But angle the trim tab just slightly, and it creates a low-pressure zone that pulls the rudder, which turns the ship.

You don't have to change everything. You just have to find the high-leverage point.

I am the trim tab for my lineage. My grandfather was lost in his neurodivergence. My father struggled with his. I nearly destroyed myself with mine. But I found the angle. I made the adjustment.

My children won't have to suffer forty years to understand their own minds.

That's the trim tab effect.
