### 3.2 Forensic Constraint Analysis: The Octal PSRAM "Kill Zone"

The most critical engineering challenge in the Phenix Navigator is the conflict between the **Octal PSRAM (OPI)** and the GPIO requirements of the LoRa radio. Failure to respect these constraints results in immediate boot loops and memory corruption.

#### The Octal PSRAM Conflict

The "R8" designation in ESP32-S3R8 indicates 8MB of PSRAM connected via an 8-line Octal SPI bus. This high-speed memory is essential for buffering the 320×480 display frames without tearing. However, the OPI interface permanently consumes specific GPIO pins.

**Forbidden Pins (The Kill Zone):** `GPIO 33, 34, 35, 36, 37`

**Mechanism:** These pins are physically bonded to the internal PSRAM die within the System-in-Package (SiP). They are electrically unavailable for any external peripheral connection.

> ⚠️ **WARNING:** Attempting to route the LoRa radio's SPI lines (MOSI/MISO/CLK) or Control lines (CS/RST) to these pins will cause immediate bus contention, random kernel panics during PSRAM access, or display artifacts.

#### The QSPI Display Bottleneck

The "Type B" board uses the AXS15231B controller over a Quad SPI (QSPI) interface to achieve 60FPS. This consumes another block of high-speed pins, typically GPIO 39-42 for data lanes, plus control lines. This high-density utilization leaves a very narrow window of available pins for the SX1262 LoRa transceiver.
