## The Core Challenge

**As an LLC, donations received are taxable business income and are not tax-deductible for donors.**

This single structural fact shapes every strategic choice about donation infrastructure, because the crypto philanthropy ecosystem — where **The Giving Block processed over $1 billion in 2024** with an average donation of **$10,978** — flows almost exclusively to 501(c)(3) organizations. High-net-worth crypto donors specifically seek tax-advantaged giving.

## The Structural Options

### Option 1: Fiscal Sponsorship (Recommended Starting Point)

**Partner with established 501(c)(3) fiscal sponsor**

**Pros:**
- Continue operating as LLC
- Accept tax-deductible donations through sponsor
- Lower complexity than forming 501(c)(3)
- Faster to implement

**Cons:**
- 5-10% fee typical
- Less control over donation process
- Dependent on sponsor relationship

**Best Fit: Open Collective**
- Full financial transparency (every transaction public)
- Collective budgeting tools
- Handles taxes/1099s
- GitHub Sponsors integration (zero fees)
- Well-suited for open-source projects

**Other Options:**
- Open Source Collective (501(c)(6), for open-source)
- Hack Club
- Software Freedom Conservancy

### Option 2: Companion 501(c)(3)

**Form separate nonprofit entity**

**Pros:**
- Full control over donation process
- Direct tax-deductible status
- Can accept all donation types

**Cons:**
- Higher complexity (two entities to manage)
- Longer setup time (6-12 months)
- Ongoing compliance requirements
- Separation of business vs. charitable activities

**Structure:**
- LLC: Business operations (hardware sales, consulting, SaaS)
- 501(c)(3): Charitable donations, grants, tax-deductible giving

### Option 3: Hybrid Model (Long-term Vision)

**Keep LLC + Fiscal Sponsorship → Evaluate 501(c)(3) Later**

**Timeline:**
1. **Now**: Set up Open Collective fiscal sponsorship
2. **6 months**: Evaluate donation activity, donor base, revenue projections
3. **12 months**: Decide on 501(c)(3) formation if needed

**Revenue Streams:**
- Donations (through fiscal sponsor or 501(c)(3))
- Hardware kit sales (LLC)
- Hosted Buffer instances (LLC SaaS)
- Consulting services (LLC)
- Corporate sponsorships (LLC or 501(c)(3))

## The Crypto-Native Vision: Trimtab Protocol

### ERC-5564 Stealth Addresses

**Use Case: Anonymous Donations**

The standard's documentation cites donations as a primary use case: "individuals may wish to donate to a specific organization while preferring not to disclose a link between themselves and the recipient."

**Live Implementations:**
- **Umbra Cash** (ScopeLift): Ethereum mainnet and L2s
- **Fluidkey**: Base, Optimism, Arbitrum, Polygon, Gnosis, Ethereum

**Challenge:**
- Anonymous stealth-address donations complicate tax receipts for donors
- May create regulatory ambiguity for receiving LLC
- **Solution**: Fiscal sponsorship or 501(c)(3) handles tax compliance

## The Decision Framework

### Evaluate After 6 Months

1. **Donation Activity**
   - Volume of donations received
   - Average donation size
   - Donor base composition

2. **Revenue Projections**
   - Hardware kit sales potential
   - SaaS (hosted Buffer) revenue
   - Consulting opportunities
   - Corporate sponsorships

3. **Donor Feedback**
   - Do donors need tax deductions?
   - Are they high-net-worth individuals?
   - Crypto-native donors?

### Decision Point

**If donations are significant and donors need tax deductions:**
→ Form companion 501(c)(3)

**If hybrid revenue streams are strong:**
→ Continue with fiscal sponsorship

**If both:**
→ Hybrid model with both LLC and 501(c)(3)
