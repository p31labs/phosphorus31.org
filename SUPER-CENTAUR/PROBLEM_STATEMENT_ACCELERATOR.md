# Problem Statement: The Missing Infrastructure for Neurodivergent Minds

## 1. Scale

Neurodivergence affects 15–20% of the global population. In the United States, approximately 1 in 36 children are diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder (CDC, 2023). AuDHD co-occurrence is increasingly recognized as the norm, not the exception. These are not rare conditions requiring specialized clinical tools. These are common human experiences requiring common infrastructure.

## 2. The Gap: Two Failing Categories

Current assistive technology falls into two categories, both of which fail neurodivergent users:

**Clinical AAC/AT devices** cost $3,000–$15,000, require insurance pre-authorization, and lock users into proprietary ecosystems designed by neurotypical clinicians for neurotypical workflows. They assume stable internet connectivity, predictable environments, and the ability to navigate complex menu hierarchies. When an autistic adult experiences a sensory overload meltdown in a grocery store and cannot verbalize, a $12,000 speech-generating device that requires Wi‑Fi and a 15-minute boot sequence is not infrastructure. It is a paperweight.

**Consumer wellness apps** offer no clinical rigor, harvest user data for advertising, and depend entirely on cloud services. They are designed for productivity optimization, not cognitive accessibility. When medication timing conflicts aren't tracked and calcium levels crash in someone managing both hypoparathyroidism and AuDHD, a meditation app cannot prevent the emergency room visit. When the only communication tools require internet connectivity, a power outage or dead zone becomes a communication blackout.

## 3. The Missing Middle

There is no affordable, open-source, privacy-respecting hardware-plus-software ecosystem designed BY neurodivergent people FOR neurodivergent use cases. The market lacks tools that address:

- **Sensory regulation** during overload episodes (haptic feedback that works offline, without Bluetooth pairing)
- **Executive function support** that accounts for medication interactions (calcium supplements and stimulants require a 4-hour separation window, or absorption fails)
- **Communication during nonverbal episodes** (when the speech centers shut down, but the need to communicate remains)
- **Medication interaction management** (autism plus ADHD plus chronic medical conditions like hypoparathyroidism create interaction cascades that single-purpose tools cannot track)

The founder's lived experience managing hypoparathyroidism (calcium/phosphorus regulation) alongside AuDHD exemplifies this multi-system complexity: calcium supplements block Vyvanse absorption if taken within 4 hours, magnesium affects calcium regulation, and executive function failures during sensory overload can cascade into missed medication timing, triggering preventable medical crises. Current tools cannot track these interactions.

## 4. The Cost of Inaction

The absence of appropriate infrastructure has measurable consequences:

**Employment outcomes:** Neurodivergent adults face unemployment rates 3–4x higher than the general population. When executive function tools don't account for medication timing, when sensory regulation requires expensive clinical devices, when communication fails during stress episodes, employment becomes unsustainable.

**Relationship strain:** Caregiver burnout is epidemic. When a meltdown occurs in public and the only communication tool requires internet connectivity that isn't available, bystanders assume non-compliance or behavioral issues. The relationship damage compounds.

**Misdiagnosis:** When medication interactions aren't tracked, symptoms are misattributed to "non-compliance" rather than biochemical conflicts. Medical gaslighting becomes routine.

**Emergency room visits:** Preventable crises become acute medical events. A calcium crash from hypoparathyroidism, triggered by missed medication timing during an executive function failure, results in a $5,000 ER visit that could have been prevented by a $200 device that tracks interactions and alerts before the cascade begins.

Neurodivergence is not a disease to be cured. It is a difference that requires different infrastructure. We need tools built by people who live the problem. We need hardware that works offline. We need software that respects privacy. We need systems that account for multi-condition complexity. We need infrastructure, not inspiration.
