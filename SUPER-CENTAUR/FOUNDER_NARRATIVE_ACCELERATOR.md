# Founder Narrative: Will

For 16 years, Will maintained electrical systems for nuclear power applications as a Department of Defense civilian engineer. His work required redundancy, fault tolerance, and safety margins measured in multiple layers. When power distribution failures could compromise safety-critical operations, the engineering discipline becomes non-negotiable. He specialized in motor controls, safety-critical infrastructure, and systems that protect lives.

In May 2025, he left that position. In the same year, at age 39, he received a diagnosis that recontextualized everything: AuDHD, the co-occurrence of autism and ADHD. Every workaround he'd built over two decades, every "why can't you just" moment, every system he'd constructed to function in unaccommodated workplaces—these weren't character flaws or personal failures. They were undiagnosed neurodivergent adaptations.

The diagnosis arrived alongside a second reality he's managed since 2003: hypoparathyroidism, a rare condition affecting fewer than 200,000 people in the United States. His body cannot properly regulate calcium and phosphorus, the two elements that form hydroxyapatite—the mineral matrix of bone and teeth, and the structural foundation of cellular energy. Without precise medication timing and constant metabolic awareness, calcium levels crash, triggering preventable medical crises.

The intersection of these conditions creates cascading failures that existing assistive technology cannot address. Calcium supplements block Vyvanse absorption if taken within four hours. Executive function failures during sensory overload can cascade into missed medication timing. Current tools track medications in isolation or executive function in isolation, but not the interaction between autism, ADHD, and chronic medical conditions.

He named the organization Phosphorus-31 after the only stable isotope of phosphorus: the backbone of ATP, a structural component of DNA, the element that fires every neural signal. It's also one of the two elements his body cannot properly regulate. The name is both metaphor and medical reality.

P31 Labs exists because the tools don't exist yet. He's not building this because he studied the market. He's building it because he needed it, couldn't find it, and has the engineering background to make it real. The same systems thinking that maintains safety-critical electrical infrastructure—redundancy, fault tolerance, offline operation, no single point of failure—applies to a completely different domain: protecting minds instead of protecting lives.

As a father of two children, ages 9 and 6, the mission extends beyond personal need. The assistive technology market serves clinical devices priced at $3,000–$15,000 or consumer apps that harvest data and depend on cloud services. There is no affordable, open-source, privacy-respecting ecosystem designed by neurodivergent people for neurodivergent use cases. P31 Labs is building that infrastructure.

The engineering discipline remains the same. The domain has shifted. The systems that maintain safety-critical infrastructure—redundancy, fault tolerance, offline operation—are the same systems that keep a neurodivergent mind stable. When the neutral fails, the entire system collapses. The mesh holds.
