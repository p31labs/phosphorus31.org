# Defensive Publication Strategy — Accelerator Application Summary

P31 Labs employs defensive publication via Zenodo (DOI assignment) as our primary IP strategy, establishing timestamped prior art for core innovations: LoRa mesh networking for assistive technology communication, haptic feedback patterns for sensory regulation in neurodivergent users, communication voltage assessment algorithms, and SIC-POVM geometry applied to secure device communication. All publications are licensed under Apache 2.0, ensuring community freedom to build.

For a nonprofit serving neurodivergent communities, defensive publication is strategically superior to patents. Patents create exclusionary barriers that lock assistive technology behind licensing fees and corporate gatekeepers—precisely the Wye topology dependency we reject. By publishing first, we prevent patent trolls from claiming ownership while guaranteeing that anyone can fork, adapt, and launch these tools without permission. This aligns with our Delta topology principle: no single point of failure, no vendor lock-in, no institutional dependency.

Accelerator reviewers should recognize this as a strength, not a weakness. We're not avoiding patents due to cost constraints; we're choosing radical openness as an offensive strategy. When innovations are published and timestamped, they cannot be patented by competitors. This protects the entire neurodivergent community from locked ecosystems while establishing P31 Labs as the canonical source of prior art. The mesh holds because the code is free, the documentation is public, and the community owns the tools.
